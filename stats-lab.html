00 — the experiment

What we actually compared

The intuition. We want to know one thing: do skwad's multi-agent code reviews score higher than a single CI agent's — and can we trust that answer? Everything downstream exists to make that answer defensible.

The design. We took 8 real pull requests. For each PR, both systems produced a code review. A neutral judge model (gpt-5.4) then scored every review against 7 criteria, each on a 0–3 scale (so a perfect review tops out at 21 points). To remove luck and order-effects, the judge ran 3 counterbalanced, A/B-blinded times per review — the judge never knew which system was "A" and which was "B." The per-criterion score is the median of those 3 runs.

turn the judge's raw ratings into one trustworthy score per criterion — the 3-run median vote.

01 — scoring & the median vote

From three judge runs to one score

The intuition. The judge scored each review three times. Three runs means three slightly different opinions. We need to collapse them into one number per criterion without letting a single weird run hijack the result. The tool for that is the median — line the three values up in order and take the middle one. The middle value can't be dragged around by an extreme; that's the whole point.

The formula. Per criterion, score = median(run1, run2, run3). The code uses statistics.median_low (judge.py:415). A review's total is the sum across all 7 criteria: total = Σ score.

Worked example — skwad on watson #890 (total = 2), and the reason we vote

Showing the median vote (what we actually use).

On Severity Accuracy, the three runs were [0, 2, 0]. One run spiked to a 2; the other two said 0. The median is 0 — it throws the spike away. The mean would be 0.67, quietly importing that noisy run into the score. A single jittery run can't inflate anything when you vote by median.

The "_low" footnote — don't overthink it. With 3 runs (an odd count) the middle value is unambiguous, so median_low behaves like a plain median. The "low" tiebreak only matters when you have an even number of runs and need to pick between two middles. For us it never changes anything.

before comparing skwad to CI, ask whether those three runs even agreed — the trust gate, Krippendorff's α.

02 — the trust gate, checked first

Krippendorff's α — do the runs agree?

The intuition. A score is only worth analyzing if the judge is consistent. If the 3 counterbalanced runs disagree wildly, the "score" is just noise wearing a number. So before we test whether skwad beats CI, we measure agreement across the 3 runs and gate on it. This sits under everything else.

The formula. α = 1 − (observed disagreement / expected-by-chance disagreement). α=1 is perfect agreement, α=0 is chance, α<0 is systematic disagreement. We use the ordinal version: a 2-vs-3 disagreement barely counts, a 0-vs-3 disagreement counts a lot — distance on the scale is weighted.

The exact gate rule: we do not gate on the point estimate of α. We bootstrap a 95% confidence interval for α and require its lower bound ≥ 0.60. The authoritative gate runs that check on the headline path — compute_inter_run_alpha → krippendorff_alpha_ordinal(run_scores, seed=0) (stats.py:281, n_boot=2000). The §6 table also carries a displayed pass/fail flag (reporter.py:529) computed from a lighter bootstrap — same ≥ 0.60 rule, different resamples; see the caveat below. Gating on the pessimistic end is deliberately conservative — reliability has to hold up even on a bad day. What's "agreeing" here = the 3 counterbalanced judge runs.

Where these cells come from. The α and CI numbers above are static HTML — transcribed by hand from Python's research-report.md §6 (produced by reporter.py:_sec6_judge_consistency). Nothing on this page recomputes α in the browser; it mirrors the one baked run.

How the number is actually produced. We don't hand-roll the α arithmetic — it's a single library call, krippendorff.alpha(data_arr, level_of_measurement="ordinal") (stats.py:194, package krippendorff>=0.6.0). The α = 1 − Do/De machinery lives inside that package; we just hand it a grid and read one number back.

What goes in the grid. The raters are the 3 judge runs (run_scores = 3 rows, stats.py:254). The items (units) are every (PR × system) pair — skwad's and CI's scores are pooled into one list, so the pilot's 8 PRs give 16 items per criterion. α is computed once per criterion (the 7 rows above): it measures run-to-run consistency of the judge, agnostic to which system was reviewed. It is not per-system and not a single overall number.

Diagram 1 — one α-grid per criterion · raters vs items vs criteria

The core "what's what." One grid like this exists per criterion — 7 in all (the tabs up top). Within a criterion the rows are the 3 judge runs (the raters) and each column is one item: a (PR × system) pair, with skwad's and CI's scores pooled into the same 16-column list. α is computed down the columns — do the 3 runs agree? — giving one number for that criterion (here Severity Accuracy = 0.4963).

Why "ordinal". On the 0–3 scale a 2-vs-3 disagreement barely counts while 0-vs-3 counts a lot. The ordinal distance weights disagreements by how far apart they are on the scale, so near-misses don't tank the score the way they would under a nominal (exact-match only) metric.

Diagram 2 — ordinal distance: not all disagreements cost the same

Why "ordinal". Disagreements are penalized by how far apart they sit on the 0–3 scale. A 2-vs-3 near-miss barely dents α; a 0-vs-3 — or watson#890's 0-vs-2 (Δ2) spike — costs a lot. That weighting is exactly what level_of_measurement="ordinal" turns on (stats.py:194); the precise distance math lives inside the krippendorff package.

The confidence interval. The CI is a bootstrap: resample the items (columns) with replacement, recompute α on each resample, and take the 2.5th / 97.5th percentiles (stats.py:196–211). The point α is deterministic; only the interval comes from resampling. The authoritative gate then checks the lower bound ≥ 0.60 on the headline path (compute_inter_run_alpha → stats.py:281, seed=0, n_boot=2000); the §6 table additionally shows a displayed pass/fail flag (reporter.py:529) from a lighter n_boot=200 bootstrap — same threshold, see the caveat below. Degenerate cases are handled: fewer than 2 items → α is undefined; all-identical scores → treated as perfect agreement (pass).

Seven gates, one per criterion — and they stand alone. There are 7 α's, one for each criterion (stats.py:252); there is no per-system α and no single overall α. Unlike the quality scores — which are summed across the 7 criteria into the 21-point total (judge.py:443) — the reliability α's are never combined: each criterion gets its own independent pass/fail against its own CI-lower ≥ 0.60 (pilot.py:154). The 0.60 bar is itself a chosen, relaxed threshold (ALPHA_GATE = 0.6, pilot.py:27; methodology.md only calls it "acceptable", with no citation) — it sits below Krippendorff's published conventions (≥ 0.667 "tentatively acceptable", ≥ 0.80 "reliable"). The one principled part is where it's applied: to the conservative bootstrap CI lower bound, not the point estimate. The per-criterion gates then roll up into a single harness-validity flag — pilot_pass requires every measurable criterion to clear the bar.

Diagram 3 — how the grid becomes one α

α = 1 − Dₒ/Dₑ. Observed disagreement Dₒ is how much the 3 runs actually differ; expected Dₑ is how much they'd differ by chance given the score spread. When scores barely vary, Dₑ shrinks, so even a little Dₒ makes α collapse — that's why Severity Accuracy lands at 0.4963 while Coverage (near-identical runs, wider spread) sits at 0.9667.

trust established on the criteria that survived — now the primary question: is skwad actually better? Wilcoxon signed-rank.

03 — the primary test · "is skwad better?"

The Wilcoxon signed-rank test

The intuition. Our design is paired: each PR gets both a skwad total and a CI total, side by side. Look at the differences (skwad − CI). If the two systems were really equal, those differences would scatter randomly around zero — some positive, some negative. If skwad is genuinely better, they pile up on the positive side. Wilcoxon measures exactly how lopsided that pile is, using only the ranks of the differences (not their raw sizes — robust to outliers and fine on small/ordinal data).

The formula. wilcoxon(diffs, zero_method="wilcox", alternative="two-sided") with no continuity correction (stats.py:55). Rank the absolute differences, sum the ranks of the positive ones (W⁺) and the negative ones (W⁻); the statistic is the smaller pile.

Worked on our 8 PR totals — step through it

—W⁺ (ranks where skwad ahead)
—W⁻ (ranks where CI ahead)
—statistic = min(W⁺, W⁻)
—two-sided p-value
Reading our result: all 8 differences are positive — skwad won every single PR — so W⁻ = 0, W⁺ = 36, the statistic is 0.0, and the exact two-sided p = 0.0078. If the systems were truly equal, getting all 8 PRs leaning the same way is about as extreme as 8 coin flips all landing heads.
One honest nuance: the absolute differences contain ties (two 10s, two 1s), which usually forces an approximate p-value. Here it doesn't — and not by luck. All 8 differences share the same sign, so the one-sided probability of "all 8 lean one way" is exactly (1/2)⁸ = 1/256 ≈ 0.0039. A two-sided test also counts the equally-extreme mirror image — "all 8 lean the other way" — doubling it to 2/256 = 0.0078125. The tie-affected magnitudes never enter either tail, so the exact value is rigorously valid, not a coincidence of scipy's auto mode. We report it as 0.0078.

The trap to memorize: p < 0.05 does not mean "95% chance skwad is better." It means "if there were no difference, data this extreme would show up under 5% of the time." It's a statement about the data under the null — not the probability our hypothesis is true.

p says whether there's a difference. now measure how big — Cliff's δ.

04 — effect size · "how big is it?"

Cliff's delta (δ)

The intuition. A p-value never tells you the size of an effect — only whether one exists. Cliff's δ measures size by pure dominance: line up every skwad score against every CI score and ask how often skwad wins minus how often it loses.

δ = P(skwad > CI) − P(skwad < CI) = (wins − losses) / total  →  ranges from −1 to +1

Magnitude thresholds (Romano et al.): |δ| < 0.147 negligible · < 0.33 small · < 0.474 medium · ≥ 0.474 large. Our δ = 0.7812 = 50/64 net wins — comfortably large.

Why δ and not Cohen's d? Cohen's d is built from means and standard deviations — parametric. Cliff's δ is built from ordering, so it matches our rank-based Wilcoxon and our ordinal scores. Don't mix a parametric effect size with a non-parametric test.

we have a size — now bound our confidence in it. BCa bootstrap confidence interval.

05 — confidence · "how sure are we?"

The BCa bootstrap interval

The intuition. δ = 0.7812 is a single guess from one sample of 8 PRs. A confidence interval gives the range of plausible truths. But how do you get a range from one dataset? You fake having many: resample your PRs with replacement (some appear twice, some not at all), recompute δ, and repeat thousands of times. That cloud of δ's is an empirical picture of the uncertainty.

The formula. bootstrap(..., method="BCa", n_resamples=2000, confidence_level=0.95) (stats.py:145). Crucially this is not the plain percentile bootstrap. BCa = Bias-Corrected and accelerated — it makes two corrections before choosing which percentiles to read off:

  • Bias correction (z₀): if the bootstrap cloud isn't centered on the observed δ, shift to compensate.
  • Acceleration (a): correct for skew — when the statistic's spread changes with the true value (measured by leaving each PR out one at a time, the "jackknife").

One detail: the upper bound sits exactly at 1.00 because δ mathematically maxes out at 1 — it's the ceiling of the scale, not a coincidence. (Note we did not just read off the 2.5/97.5 percentiles; BCa adjusts those endpoints via z₀ and the jackknife acceleration first.)

test, size, and interval are in. how do we combine them into a single yes/no? the verdict rule.

06 — the verdict rule

The AND-gate for "skwad wins"

The intuition. Significance alone isn't enough (a trivial effect can be significant), and a big effect alone isn't enough (it could be noise). The report demands both. The hypothesis "skwad is better" (H1) is supported only if the result is statistically significant and the effect is at least medium-sized.

The formula (reporter.py:672): H1 supported ⟺ p < 0.05 AND |δ| ≥ 0.33

Plug in our numbers

Both gates pass → H1 supported: skwad reviews are significantly and substantially better than the single CI agent.

If either gate had failed — say a strong δ but p = 0.20, or p = 0.001 but δ = 0.10 — the verdict would be "not supported." Requiring both is what stops us from over-claiming.

the headline verdict is settled. now the careful part: we ran many extra tests — how do we keep them honest? Benjamini–Hochberg FDR.

07 — many tests, kept honest

Benjamini–Hochberg FDR

The intuition. After the headline, we dig in: run a separate test on each of the 7 criteria, plus the Easy and Hard difficulty buckets — 9 tests. The danger: every test at 5% has a 5% false-alarm rate even when nothing is real. Run 9 and the chance of at least one false positive is large. BH controls the false-discovery proportion — of the findings you flag, on average ≤ q of them are false.

The formula (stats.py:315): false_discovery_control(method="bh") at q = 0.05. It's a step-up procedure: sort the p-values, and compare each to a rank-scaled threshold. It is not Bonferroni — we do not just multiply each p by m.

Why m = 9, not 10? 7 criteria + Easy + Hard. The Medium bucket is dropped: only N = 1 PR landed there, and the test code requires len ≥ 2 (reporter.py:579). Silently it would look like we tested 10 things — we didn't.

Our 9 exploratory tests. Drag q and watch which survive.

q (allowed false-discovery rate): 0.05
—"significant" if you ignore the problem (raw p < q)
—survive BH at this q
Teaching gold, on our real data: at the standard q = 0.05, four criteria have raw p = 0.0312 (they look significant) — but none of the 9 survive BH correction (the smallest adjusted p is 0.0703). That's the multiple-comparisons honesty lesson in one screen. The headline verdict in §06 still stands — these per-criterion tests are exploratory, hypothesis-generating, not the pre-registered headline.

Why BH and not Bonferroni? Bonferroni controls the chance of any false positive (FWER) — safe but brutal, it hides real effects. BH controls the expected proportion of false discoveries among your hits — more power, the right tool for screening.

08 — robustness side-checks

Are the criteria redundant? Did blinding work?

(a) Spearman inter-criterion correlation

The intuition. If two criteria always move together, they're measuring the same thing and one is redundant. Spearman correlation measures monotonic agreement between the ranks of two criteria (it asks "when one goes up, does the other tend to go up?" — about ranks, not a straight line) (reporter.py:500, scipy.spearmanr). We compute it for all 7×7 criterion pairs.

The intuition. The judge saw reviews in slots A and B. If it secretly favored whichever review sat in slot A, our blinding failed. We run a Wilcoxon on slot-A vs slot-B scores, once per system (reporter.py:599). We want these to be non-significant — no detectable slot preference.

the one paragraph to say in the room

"First we confirmed the judge was consistent — four of seven criteria cleared our Krippendorff's α trust gate on the CI lower bound; the noisy three are flagged as a caveat, not dropped. skwad won all 8 PRs on the full 21-point total: a paired Wilcoxon gives p = 0.0078, a Cliff's δ of 0.78 (large), and a BCa 95% CI of [0.50, 1.00] that excludes zero. Means: 14.6 vs 7.6 out of 21."

Two things to sanity-check in our setup, not the textbook:
1. The data is genuinely paired — the same 8 PRs through both systems — which is what licenses the signed-rank test.
2. Differences are computed skwad − CI, so a positive δ means skwad wins. Flipping that sign is the one truly embarrassing mistake.
